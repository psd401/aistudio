## Identifying the Transformational Opportunity

Opportunity A From the anchor chart · System change by design thinking

### Building the Open Adaptive District

Description

Peninsula is building a new way of working — a district that can keep pace with AI as it changes. Right now, when a new tool comes out, school districts often take years to figure out what to do with it. By then, the next wave has already arrived, and educators are exhausted. We are going to change that here.

Every six weeks, small teams across the district — cabinet departments, principal teams by grade level, and later groups of teachers and operations staff — will choose one experiment to try. They will start by asking what has changed in the last six weeks and what problem they want to solve. They will write down what they expect to happen and how they will know. They will run the experiment. At the end of the cycle, they will share what they learned — including what did not work — and everything will be posted publicly on psd401.ai so families, staff, and other districts can see how we work. (We call this the SHIP cycle.)

Each quarter, I will write a short, three-sentence statement of what we are after, why it matters now, and what we will not give up along the way. Teams take that direction and choose their own path inside it. I do not tell teams how to do their work — I tell them what we are after. A template guides this quarterly statement.

Rather than adding more meetings to coordinate all of this, we are building an internal AI tool that quietly reads each team's weekly written updates and keeps everyone — including me — informed about what is working, what is stuck, and where teams could help each other. The point is simple: a district learning to use AI well should use AI to organize its own learning.

What we are building is not an AI plan or a technology rollout. It is the district's ability to keep adjusting, honestly and in public, at the pace the world is actually moving — so that what we offer our students next year can be very different from what we offered them last year, and we can show our families exactly how we got there.

Why this is important

AI capability is doubling on a roughly six-month curve while traditional district change cycles run two to five years. Without an adaptive operating model, every new wave of technology creates fresh paralysis. Districts default to one of two failure modes: waiting for the state to act, or accepting vendor-driven adoption that bypasses public mission. Both deepen inequity and surrender district agency. The Open Adaptive District is the alternative.

Barriers

- Traditional change models (Kotter, Fullan) assume stable end-states and are too slow for an exponential environment
- Cabinet, principals, and staff prioritize what they hear most often; the operating model competes with forty other initiatives
- Meeting culture struggles to carry cross-silo coordination
- Some leaders treat complex problems as if they were complicated and try to expert-solve them
- Education norms punish public failure, which discourages honest debriefs

Risks

- Moving fast without governance erodes community trust
- Moving slowly cedes the field to vendors and consultants
- Capacity stays in four or five senior people instead of a system
- Cycles become theater — teams perform SHIP without actually shipping
- Bets that don't work get quietly dropped instead of publicly debriefed

Resources needed

- Superintendent ownership and cabinet alignment (in place)
- The Logbook (the CIO builds summer 2026)
- Quarterly intent discipline from the superintendent
- Dedicated time for SHIP teams — not bolt-on
- Communications capacity to make wins and decommissions visible
- A published playbook on psd401.ai

How have you tried to address this

Four years of progressive AI work at PSD: a growing roster of production AI agents we have built ourselves, a custom AI tools platform at **psd401.ai/aistudio** where staff create their own assistants, the public **psd401.ai** website where we share our work openly, and national-level conference and press visibility.

**What has worked:** top-down permission paired with bottom-up tinkering across cabinet, principals, and staff; making our work visible early through psd401.ai, public talks, and press coverage; building tools that staff actually use.

**What hasn't worked yet:** assuming directors would prioritize without being told; assuming principals would notice and replicate without scaffolding; treating AI as a teaching-and-learning topic rather than a district operating-model question.

**What remains underbuilt:** the *cadence* — a published, repeatable adaptive cycle that other leaders can run without being us. The Open Adaptive District is that cadence.

Opportunity B From the anchor chart · Innovation at scale

### The Open Adaptive District as a Public-Interest Model

Description

The work happening inside Peninsula is too important to keep to ourselves. Other districts — large and small, well-resourced and not — face the same questions we do, but very few have the staff, the partners, or the permission to work them out on their own. Without somewhere credible to look, those districts often end up buying whatever a vendor is selling. We want there to be a public alternative they can turn to — one built by districts, for districts.

Across the fellowship year, we will turn Peninsula's working model into something other districts can actually pick up and use. Our public website, psd401.ai, will grow from a place where we tell stories of our work into a working library — the six-week cycle we run, the templates we use, the rules we follow about what we share and what we keep private, the AI tools we build, and the honest debrief from every team, including the experiments that failed. If another district could copy it, adapt it, or run it themselves, it will be there.

In February 2027, Peninsula will host a regional gathering for superintendents and their teams from across the Pacific Northwest. The day will not be a conference. It will be a working meeting, where each district leaves with a draft of their own first six-week cycle, a partner district to compare notes with through the spring, and clear ways to keep contributing back to the shared library as they go. We hope at least three or four other districts are running their own cycle by the end of the school year.

Alongside the gathering, we will publish a longer written case study describing how Peninsula has done this work and what we have learned. A shorter, more practical whitepaper will give district leaders the tools they need to start. A short visual piece — a video or photo essay — will help families and staff see the spirit of the work, not just the structure.

We are not trying to put a flag on the moon. We are trying to make sure that, when the next wave of AI lands, public schools have a model of their own to point to — one that other districts had a hand in building.

Why this is important

Capacity-constrained districts will otherwise be absorbed into closed vendor stacks. A public, district-owned alternative protects public education's agency in deciding what AI in education looks like. Districts deserve a seat at the table, not a seat in the audience. Peninsula's local work becomes a public contribution.

Barriers

- Regional districts have very different readiness levels
- Sustained inter-district collaboration is hard without immediate value
- PSD capacity is already stretched leading internally
- No widely shared district-centered AI operating framework currently exists for the coalition to rally around
- Other districts may pattern-match this to past "leader district shows off" efforts and disengage

Risks

- Coalition becomes conversation without artifacts
- PSD overextends and weakens the internal work
- Equity concern: early-mover districts benefit faster than capacity-constrained ones
- Framing becomes too oppositional toward vendors and burns relationships

Resources needed

- Convening capacity for the February symposium
- psd401.ai evolved into a two-way contribution platform
- Writing and publication time for the long-form article and whitepaper
- Higher education and workforce partnerships
- Fellowship network connections
- Modest funding for symposium and publication production

How have you tried to address this

The primary national platform for sharing this work has been the **American Association of School Administrators (AASA)**, where the superintendent sits alongside peers in national conversations on AI, governance, and operations. Beyond AASA, Peninsula engages prominently through **CoSN** (the Consortium for School Networking) and **Digital Promise's League of Innovative Schools**, with smaller participation in **ERDI** and **WASA**. Public-facing sharing through psd401.ai, published articles, and national conference talks has reached district leaders well beyond those formal networks. Individual districts have reached out one at a time for informal mentoring.

**What has worked:** districts across the country have been inspired by Peninsula's example — including at least one that stood up a full AI department after seeing our work. Peninsula has earned a national platform.

**What hasn't worked yet:** sharing has been one-way; there is no coalition structure; no shared framework for other districts to fork.

Part 02

## Action Planning

### Idea

Launch and document **The Open Adaptive District** — an operating model in which cross-silo teams run six-week SHIP cycles (Sense, Hypothesize, Implement, Publish) under a quarterly superintendent intent, coordinated through an The Logbook (an AI agent that reads team Spaces and synthesizes across the district), with every cycle's lessons published openly on psd401.ai. Convene a February 2027 regional symposium and co-publish a long-form article and whitepaper framing PSD's operating model as a portable, public-interest contribution other districts can adopt.

### How this addresses both opportunities

A and B are two layers of the same work. **A is the internal practice; B is the field-facing translation.** Every internal artifact becomes a public artifact. SHIP cycles produce the evidence that the operating model works; psd401.ai publishes that evidence as the field's reference implementation. The February symposium activates the coalition. The publication makes the model citable.

## TregoED SCAN Protocol

### S — See the issues

The district is operating in a moment when AI is transforming learning, work, communication, and public expectations on a months-long curve. The issue is not whether AI will be used in schools; it is whether districts will build the institutional capacity to govern, integrate, and direct AI responsibly — and whether public education will define its own model before closed vendor stacks define one for it.

### C — Clarify the issues

**"Adaptive District"** means a district whose decision and execution loops match the pace of the change it operates in. **"Public-interest model"** means an approach centered on equity, transparency, interoperability, and district agency — distinct from market dependence.
