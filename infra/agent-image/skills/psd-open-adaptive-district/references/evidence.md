# The Evidence Behind SHIP

A plain-language summary of the research behind the methods the SHIP cycle borrows — and where to be cautious.

**128** open-access studies · 9 method families · education · healthcare · technology · business · scoping review, June 2026

## ✓ What the evidence supports

- Short, iterative cycles are the right tool for fast-moving, uncertain problems — the repeated finding across agile, improvement science, and complexity research.
- A hypothesis with a measurable success criterion is what turns activity into learning (Lean Startup, PDSA, online experimentation).
- Psychological safety and openly debriefing failure are well-supported conditions for organizational learning (Edmondson; team-learning research).
- Leadership setting intent while teams own the method — “directed opportunism” — has both theory and evidence behind it (dynamic capabilities, agile).
- Capability builds over repeated cycles; teams calibrate after the first two or three, so judge SHIP over many cycles, not the first.

## ⚠ Where to be cautious

- Rigor beats ritual: most real-world PDSA / quality-improvement work is run at low fidelity — the measurable prediction is the step teams skip.
- Multi-team improvement “collaboratives” show modest, inconsistent effects, gated by real leadership support.
- Agile and lean carry more enthusiasm than controlled evidence; much of their authority is practitioner lore.
- Almost none of this evidence comes from K-12 district operations — applying it to central-office work is a reasoned bet, not a proven result.
- Improvement methods can widen equity gaps if gains land with already-advantaged groups — a live risk for an AI rollout.

### What the evidence says about SHIP’s design choices

Six headline categories, each with a quick read of the evidence and a few studies to pull from the set below.

01

#### Short, iterative cycles

SHIP's six-week cadence rests on the most consistent finding in the literature: short, iterative cycles beat long plan-then-execute approaches when conditions change faster than plans can hold.

Agile reviews show iterative, cross-functional work fits fast-moving, uncertain problems, and the same logic underlies PDSA and rapid-cycle improvement. Caveat: teams are over-optimistic at first and only calibrate after about three cycles — treat the first cycles as practice, not yield.

Key studiesAbrahamsson 2017 ↗Vidgen 2009 ↗Mahnic 2011 ↗

02

#### Hypothesis-driven bets

A SHIP “bet” earns its keep only when it states a measurable prediction; that discipline is what turns activity into learning.

PDSA and Lean Startup both work when a falsifiable success criterion is set — and reviews find that is exactly the step most teams skip (only 4% of PDSA projects met all criteria). Theory and judgment, not blind trial-and-error, should choose which bets to run.

Key studiesTaylor 2013 ↗Knudsen 2019 ↗Felin 2019 ↗Kaufman 2017 ↗

03

#### Cross-silo team structure

SHIP runs on small, cross-functional teams that each own one bet — a structure the evidence supports, with leadership support as the decisive ingredient.

Across quality-improvement collaboratives, active leadership and management support predict impact more than team composition; team learning depends on building shared mental models; and agile teams work as self-organizing systems balancing exploration and exploitation.

Key studiesVersteeg 2012 ↗Van den Bossche 2010 ↗Vidgen 2009 ↗

04

#### Leadership intent, team-owned method

SHIP's quarterly intent — leaders set what and why, teams own how — has both theory and evidence behind it.

In high-velocity environments the most effective capabilities are simple, experiential rules that enable fast local decisions — a direct endorsement of a 3-sentence intent plus a short cycle. Strategy also creates value at the execution stage, not the planning stage.

Key studiesEisenhardt 2000 ↗Teece 2007 ↗Cortimiglia 2015 ↗

05

#### Psychological safety & honest debriefs

SHIP's “publishing failure is a feature” doctrine depends on psychological safety — the best-supported idea in this entire set.

Psychological safety is a well-established enabler of speaking up, learning, and innovation; structured debriefs and after-action reviews convert experience into learning when done with candor and discipline.

Key studiesEdmondson 2018 ↗Newman 2017 ↗Kolbe 2019 ↗Stoto 2019 ↗

06

#### Publishing & learning across teams

SHIP's Logbook and open briefs aim to make learning travel across teams — supported by networked-improvement and experiment-at-scale evidence.

Networked improvement communities accelerate learning by sharing across teams; organizations that scaled experimentation built a shared repository of both successes and failures; and lasting change requires double-loop learning — revisiting the intent itself, not just the method.

Key studiesBryk 2015 ↗Fabijan 2017 ↗Williams 2018 ↗

### The evidence, family by family

01Improvement Science, PDSA & Rapid-Cycle Quality Improvement21 sources

This literature is SHIP's closest structural cousin and its sternest critic. PDSA is exactly SHIP's inner loop, but the evidence warns that the loop fails when run sloppily. A systematic review found most published PDSA work omits the core features that make it work, iterative cycles, small-scale testing, and prediction-based change (Taylor et al., 2013); a review of 120 projects found 98% self-reported improvement but only 27% set and met a quantitative aim and just 4% met all four PDSA criteria (Knudsen et al., 2019). The lesson for SHIP is direct: the measurable bet and the explicit prediction are not bureaucratic overhead, they are what separates learning from theater. Done rigorously, PDSA is a structured experimental method, and the "problem with PDSA" is low-fidelity use, not the method (Reed & Card, 2016). Implementation science adds that proven effectiveness never guarantees uptake (Bauer & Kirchner, 2019), and frameworks like CFIR (Damschroder et al., 2009) and the ERIC strategy compilation (Powell et al., 2015) name the contextual levers SHIP teams must work. The education translation, Bryk's networked-improvement principles, supplies SHIP's grammar almost verbatim: user-centered problems, attention to variation, measurement, disciplined inquiry, and shared learning across teams (Bryk et al., 2015; Penuel et al., 2020). Two cautions: collaboratives show inconsistent effects unless leadership and management support are real (Versteeg et al., 2012; Zamboni et al., 2020; Khare & Kanyal, 2024), and quality methods can widen equity gaps if gains accrue to already-advantaged groups (Lion & Raphael, 2015), a salient risk for an AI rollout.

02Agile, Scrum & Iterative/Sprint Development12 sources

Agile is the strongest external warrant for SHIP's six-week cadence. Comparative reviews find iterative, cross-functional cycles outperform plan-driven approaches precisely in dynamic, fast-changing environments (Moniruzzaman & Hossain, 2013; Abrahamsson et al., 2017), the condition AI creates for districts. A complex-adaptive-systems study of agile teams explains *why* short cycles plus light central direction work, through coevolution, self-organization, and a deliberate balance of exploration and exploitation (Vidgen & Wang, 2009), which is the structural argument for quarterly intent plus team-owned "how." Two findings temper expectations. First, teams new to iterative estimation start over-optimistic and only stabilize after roughly three cycles (Mahnic, 2011), so Peninsula should expect its first cycles to be calibration, not yield. Second, practitioners constantly adapt Scrum rather than adopt it whole (Diebold et al., 2015), and the framework transfers beyond software, into construction and product development, only with deliberate adaptation (Streule et al., 2016; de Borba et al., 2019). SHIP is itself such an adaptation, and the literature says that is normal and necessary, not a deviation.

03Lean Startup, Lean & Kaizen10 sources

This family most directly validates the hypothesis-driven "bet." The Lean Startup synthesis grounds build-measure-learn and validated learning in academic research (Shepherd & Gruber, 2020), and a historical review situates it within the "learning school" of strategy, reframing a venture as a series of testable hypotheses (Bortolini et al., 2018), exactly SHIP's HYPOTHESIZE step. But a critical correction matters for Peninsula: experiments should be guided by theory and judgment about *which* tests are worth running, not blind trial-and-error (Felin et al., 2019). The quarterly intent is the mechanism that supplies that judgment. Building experimentation capability is itself slow and impeded by a planning mindset and missing expertise (Weissbrod & Bocken, 2016); Lean's mixed record in healthcare shows it underdelivers when imported as tooling rather than culture (Radnor et al., 2011). On the upside, two cluster-randomized trials show kaizen-style participatory improvement raises problem-solving capacity and well-being (von Thiele Schwarz et al., 2016), evidence that bottom-up cycles can help staff rather than burden them.

04Design Thinking & Human-Centered Design14 sources

This family strengthens SENSE and the case for iteration under ambiguity. Design thinking's empathize-ideate-prototype-test loop is built for ill-defined problems where the problem itself must be discovered (Brown & Wyatt, 2010), and human-centered design's defining traits are iteration and multi-level systems thinking (Melles et al., 2020; Holeman & Kane, 2019). Most relevant to SHIP, design-based research mirrors its logic almost step for step: it starts from sensing environmental change, runs iterative cycles in real settings, and publishes both reusable artifacts and transferable knowledge (Juuti & Lavonen, 2012), and it argues that having an intent strengthens rather than compromises objectivity when studying fast-changing technology environments (Hoadley & Campos, 2022; Amiel & Reeves, 2008), a useful rebuttal to the worry that a quarterly intent biases the work. Design thinking and culture reinforce each other through hands-on experiential work (Elsbach & Stigliani, 2018), suggesting SHIP's practice will reshape district culture over time, not just outputs.

05Action Research, Lesson Study & Practitioner Inquiry12 sources

This is SHIP's native-education evidence base and the clearest support for practitioner-owned cycles. Action research is, by definition, an iterative cycle of reflection and action with stakeholders (Baum et al., 2006; Bradbury, 2015), positioning frontline staff as knowledge generators rather than implementers of others' designs (Kemmis, 2009; Lytle, 2008). Lesson study supplies the empirical backbone: short design-then-enact cycles built pedagogical knowledge for novice and veteran teachers alike (Coenders & Verhoef, 2018), and the impact depends on the *institutional structures* around the cycle, not the cycle alone (Takahashi & McDougal, 2016), reinforcing that SHIP's cadence, intent, and Logbook are load-bearing, not optional scaffolding. A grounding caution appears here too: teachers often grasp a concept but struggle to translate it into technology-integrated practice (So & Kim, 2009), so SHIP bets on AI should expect a belief-to-action gap and design support for it.

06Dynamic Capabilities & Strategy Execution13 sources

Teece supplies SHIP's theoretical spine. Sensing, seizing, and reconfiguring are the microfoundations of adaptation in fast-moving markets (Teece, 2007), and these capabilities are what let firms keep redesigning how they operate as technology shifts (Teece, 2018; Warner & Wäger, 2019), the explicit case for AI. Critically, Eisenhardt and Martin (2000) show that in high-velocity environments dynamic capabilities become *simple, experiential, semi-structured* routines emphasizing rapid learning, a near-exact description of a short SHIP cycle, while Winter (2003) frames building such routines as a deliberate cost-benefit choice over ad hoc firefighting, which is the argument for institutionalizing SHIP rather than improvising. Ambidexterity research warns the core tension is balancing exploration (new bets) against exploitation (running the district), enabled by shared vision and senior-team support (Raisch et al., 2009; Jansen et al., 2008), naming the superintendent's role precisely. Business-model work locates real change at the execution stage (Cortimiglia et al., 2016), reinforcing that IMPLEMENT, not planning, is where value appears.

07Organizational Agility, Complexity & Sensemaking15 sources

This family justifies SHIP's complex-versus-complicated distinction and disciplines its ambitions. Cynefin's central claim is that strategy must match the order of complexity: complicated problems yield to expertise and good practice, but complex, novel problems require sensemaking and safe-to-fail probes rather than standard frameworks or single-loop fixes (van Beurden et al., 2013; Nachbagauer, 2021; French, 2015). AI adoption in schools is largely complex, so SHIP's probe-sense-respond logic is the right tool, and the literature's warning is that applying complicated-domain methods to complex problems backfires. A crucial qualifier from Teece, Peteraf, and Leih (2016): agility is not free and not always needed; strong dynamic capabilities are what make agility affordable, and over-investing in agility where stability suffices wastes resources. That argues for SHIP's discipline of *one* bet per team rather than perpetual churn. Empirically, agility is enabled by IT and culture but is "ill-defined" and easily over-claimed (Tallon et al., 2018; Walter, 2021; Felipe et al., 2017), so Peninsula should describe SHIP in concrete cadence-and-bet terms, not as generic "agility."

08Organizational Learning & Psychological Safety16 sources

This family is the strongest evidence for PUBLISH and for treating public failure as a feature. Psychological safety is the well-established foundation for speaking up, learning, and innovation under uncertainty (Edmondson, 2018; Newman et al., 2017; Edmondson & Bransby, 2022), and it drives learning behaviors through high-quality relationships (Carmeli et al., 2008). After-action and debrief research shows these reviews improve systems only when explicitly framed for learning, not blame (Stoto et al., 2019; Kolbe et al., 2019; Aguinis et al., 2020), the exact framing SHIP's open debrief requires. Double-loop learning supplies the deepest justification for the quarterly intent: real adaptation periodically questions the governing assumptions and decision architecture, not just the methods (Cartwright, 2002; Williams & Brown, 2018). Two honest cautions: failure to link prior learnings to future strategy stalls the whole cycle (Mitchell et al., 2012), and double-loop learning does not automatically produce reliable knowledge, it can entrench mistakes without epistemic rigor (Blackman et al., 2004). The Logbook's value is precisely to close that learning-to-strategy loop credibly.

09AI/Technology Adoption & Online Experimentation15 sources

This family speaks to SHIP's purpose, adopting AI, and to the rigor of its bets. Adoption research is consistent and sobering: trust and governance are pivotal catalysts or barriers (Hassan et al., 2024; Singh et al., 2020), which factors matter shifts by adoption stage (Neumann et al., 2022), and perceived usefulness and social influence drive whether staff actually use a tool (Venkatesh & Davis, 2000; Rogers et al., 2019). For a district, this means a "bet" cannot stop at deploying a tool; it must measure use, trust, and benefit. The online-experimentation literature is SHIP's most operational evidence: organizations like Microsoft, Booking.com, and Netflix run high volumes of controlled experiments, and the documented enablers are shared infrastructure, end-to-end team ownership, and a *central repository of both successes and failures* (Fabijan et al., 2017; Kaufman et al., 2017; Gomez-Uribe & Hunt, 2015), a near-literal blueprint for the Logbook plus open publishing. The field also stresses methodological discipline and explicit ethics governance for experimenting on real users (Larsen et al., 2023; Polonioli et al., 2023), and education-specific work urges collaboratively shaping safe generative-AI use (Baidoo-Anu & Owusu Ansah, 2023), a direct mandate for cross-silo teams.

### Where to be cautious — in depth

Open the full limitations & evidence-gaps appraisal

#### Where the evidence is weak, mixed, or contested

*\*PDSA fidelity is a documented failure point, not a footnote.* *The method SHIP leans on most — short, prediction-based cycles of change — is routinely applied badly in the field that invented it. A systematic review found most published PDSA applications fail to follow core features like iterative cycles, small-scale testing, and explicit prediction (Taylor et al., 2013). A review of 120 PDSA projects found 98% self-reported improvement, yet only 27% set and met a specific quantitative aim and just 4% of fully-documented projects met all four key PDSA features (Knudsen et al., 2019). The charitable reading is that PDSA works* when done rigorously *and the real problem is "oversimplified, low-fidelity application" (Reed & Card, 2016) — but that is precisely the risk for busy district teams. Even in a well-resourced trial, clinic staff valued PDSA's structure but struggled to measure success (Coury et al., 2017).* *Quality-improvement collaboratives — SHIP's closest organizational analog — show modest, inconsistent results.* *A realist review of 32 studies concluded the underlying evidence strength is weak (Zamboni et al., 2020). An exploratory study of 29 QI teams found* no *consistent team-characteristic predictors of impact beyond management support and active leadership (Versteeg et al., 2012). Effects, where found, concentrate at the provider level rather than the system level (Nadeem et al., 2013; Pearson et al., 2005), and a review of 28 RCTs found continuous quality improvement produced "modest, inconsistent effects" (Khare & Kanyal, 2024).* *Agile/Lean carry hype that outruns the evidence.* *Even a decade in, agile research was judged to lack a strong theoretical foundation (Dingsøyr et al., 2012), and practitioners vary Scrum so widely that "agile" describes a family of adaptations, not one validated protocol (Diebold et al., 2015). Lean's record outside manufacturing is openly questioned — "Lean in healthcare: the unfilled promise?" (Radnor et al., 2011) — and a case study found that a planning mindset and lack of lean-startup expertise stalled business experimentation (Weissbrod & Bocken, 2016).* *Design thinking is a loose construct.* *Reviewers repeatedly note its ambiguity, deriving attributes and tools precisely* because\* the term is "polysemous" (Micheli et al., 2018). Critics distinguish popularized IDEO-style design thinking from genuine participatory practice (Bjögvinsson et al., 2012). And double-loop learning — which SHIP's "decommission/continuation" debriefs assume — "does not automatically yield reliable knowledge" (Blackman et al., 2004).

#### The context-transfer gap is the central caveat

The most rigorous evidence in this corpus comes from *\*healthcare, software, and business* *— not K-12 district operations. Controlled-experiment results come from Microsoft, Booking.com, and Netflix (Fabijan et al., 2017; Kaufman et al., 2017; Gomez-Uribe & Hunt, 2015); dynamic-capabilities theory is grounded in firms (Teece, 2007; Eisenhardt & Martin, 2000); psychological-safety findings come largely from cross-sector and clinical settings (Edmondson & Bransby, 2022; Newman et al., 2017). Snowden's Cynefin is applied conceptually, not tested (van Beurden et al., 2013; French, 2015). By contrast, the* education *evidence here leans practitioner-oriented and conceptual. Lesson study studies are small and qualitative (Coenders & Verhoef, 2018, two teams; Takahashi & McDougal, 2016, a 15-school pilot); the Vermunt et al. (2019) entry lacks a usable abstract. Action research and design-based research are framed as methods and stances, not impact trials (Kemmis, 2009; Hoadley & Campos, 2022; Penuel et al., 2020), and improvement science in schools is represented largely through synthesis and review work (e.g., Bryk et al., 2015), not primary outcome data.* *No study in this corpus evaluates a SHIP-like operating model in district central-office operations.*\* We are transferring evidence across sectors, and that transfer is an assumption, not a finding.

#### Well-supported vs. largely conceptual / practitioner lore

\- *\*Reasonably supported:* *that disciplined A/B-style experimentation can be scaled in tech organizations (Fabijan et al., 2017); that psychological safety predicts learning behaviors (Carmeli et al., 2008; Newman et al., 2017); that blame-free after-action reviews aid learning (Stoto et al., 2019); that perceived usefulness drives technology adoption (Venkatesh & Davis, 2000). -* *Largely conceptual or practitioner lore:* *Cynefin's "complex vs. complicated" sorting; "directed opportunism"/quarterly-intent framing; the claim that publishing failures publicly* improves\* outcomes (supported in spirit by debrief and learning-organization writing — Edmondson, 2018; Serrat, 2017 — but not demonstrated for districts); and the synthesis of Senge/Bungay/McChrystal/Snowden itself, which appears in this corpus only indirectly.

#### Honest cautions for communication

\- Do *\*not* *claim the literature "proves" SHIP works. No study tests this model in our setting. - Do* *not* *equate "agile," "lean," or "design thinking" with validated methods — each is contested or loosely defined. - Do* *not* *cite PDSA as turnkey; the dominant finding is that fidelity usually fails (Knudsen et al., 2019; Taylor et al., 2013). - Do* *not* *promise system-level gains; QI evidence clusters at the provider/team level and is inconsistent (Zamboni et al., 2020; Khare & Kanyal, 2024). -* *Defensible claim:* *SHIP is a* theory-informed bet\* assembled from practices with promising but mixed evidence, mostly imported from other sectors, which we will test and publish honestly — including when it fails.
