# Weekly Check-In

Four short questions every team answers in their Google Chat Space — once a week, every week of the cycle.

Template· Posted weekly · ~5 minutes to write

The **Weekly Check-In** is the smallest unit of work in the SHIP cycle. Four short answers, posted in the team's Google Chat Space, once a week. It is what the Logbook reads to produce the weekly digest. It is also what end-of-cycle briefs are drafted from. Skip a week and the system goes blind.

## § I  ·  The template — Four questions, every week

Weekly Check-In Cycle \[N\] · Week \[N\] · \[Team name\]

#### ✓Tried

What concrete moves did we make this week? (Bullets fine. Dates help.)

#### ★Learned

What did we learn? What surprised us? What confirmed what we expected, and what didn't?

#### ?Stuck

What is blocking us? What are we unsure about? What might be wrong with our hypothesis?

#### →Needs

What do we need from another team, a chief, the superintendent, or the CIO? Be specific.

## § II  ·  A real example — What a good check-in looks like

Example · Communications team · Cycle 01 · Week 03

#### Tried

Drafted welcome letters using the AI assistant for 12 of the 24 incoming students this week (Sep 8–12). Tested two variations — one with school-specific paragraph appended, one without. Sent both, randomly assigned.

#### Learned

Drafting time dropped from ~28 min to ~9 min — already past our 40% target. Spanish drafts needed less revision than English ones, which surprised us. Two families replied with thank-you notes, which we didn't expect this early.

#### Stuck

We can't tell yet whether the school-specific paragraph variant outperforms — too small a sample. Also our survey response rate is sitting at 22%, lower than hoped. Worth thinking about how we measure satisfaction.

#### Needs

Could use a 15-min sync with the Tech team — heard they're working on something similar for IEPs and we might be solving the same problem twice. Also: can someone help us figure out why our survey response rate is low before next week?

## § III  ·  What makes a check-in useful — How to write one in five minutes

**Tried — name moves, not intentions.** "We piloted the tool with 12 teachers" is a move. "We thought about piloting" is not. The Logbook reads "tried" to understand what actually happened this week.

**Learned — include the surprises.** The most useful check-in lines are about what didn't go as expected. They flag for the Logbook (and the cabinet) where the hypothesis might be wrong or where something larger is going on.

**Stuck — be honest, not heroic.** "Nothing" is almost always wrong. If a team has truly nothing stuck three weeks in a row, the bet is probably too small. The Logbook surfaces patterns of "stuck" across teams.

**Needs — be specific.** "Need help" is too vague. "Need a 15-minute sync with the Tech team about IEP scheduling" lets the Logbook surface a Match flag in the next digest. The more specific the need, the faster it gets met.

## § IV  ·  The cadence — When & where

- **Where:** Posted in the team's Google Chat Space — the canonical home. Not email, not a doc, not a chief's inbox. The Logbook reads from the Space.
- **When:** Every Friday by end of day, or every Monday morning. Pick one and hold it for the cycle. Consistency matters more than which day.
- **By whom:** One team member writes it, but the whole team weighs in before it posts. Five minutes of group consensus.
- **How long:** Two or three sentences per field. If you find yourself writing essays, you're either covering the wrong week or trying too hard.

## § V  ·  How the Logbook reads it — What happens to your check-in

Every Monday, the Logbook reads the previous week's check-ins across all SHIP teams and assembles the **weekly digest**. Your "Tried" gets pattern-matched against other teams' moves. Your "Learned" feeds the running list of cycle findings. Your "Stuck" gets cross-referenced against other teams who might have a fix. Your "Needs" becomes a candidate Match flag.

Skip a check-in and the digest goes thinner. Skip two weeks and the Logbook flags the team as silent — surfaced to the team lead first, then the team's chief at 21 days.

The minimum-viable check-in On a hard week — a sick lead, a holiday, a crisis — a check-in can be as short as one sentence per field. That is still better than no check-in. The Logbook can work with little. It cannot work with nothing.
